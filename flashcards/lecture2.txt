What is an undirected graph \(G = (V, E)\) in the context of graphical models?	\(V = \{1, \ldots, m\}\) indexes the random variables \(X_1, \ldots, X_m\). An edge \(i - j \in E\) is present if the model does NOT impose \(X_i \perp\!\!\!\perp X_j \mid X_{V \setminus \{i,j\}}\). A missing edge encodes a conditional independence statement: the two variables are unrelated once all others are controlled for.
How do you read a missing edge in an undirected graphical model?	A missing edge between \(i\) and \(j\) means \(X_i \perp\!\!\!\perp X_j \mid X_{V \setminus \{i,j\}}\). For example, in a four-variable graph where 1–3 is missing, this encodes \(X_1 \perp\!\!\!\perp X_3 \mid X_2, X_4\).
What is the Global Markov Property?	Let \(A\), \(B\), \(C\) be disjoint subsets of vertices. If every path from \(A\) to \(B\) in \(G\) passes through \(C\), then \(C\) separates \(A\) and \(B\), and the Global Markov Property states \(X_A \perp\!\!\!\perp X_B \mid X_C\). This encodes more CI statements than just the pairwise ones from missing edges.
What is the relationship between the Global Markov Property and the Pairwise Markov Property?	Global Markov implies Pairwise Markov (since pairwise CI is a special case where \(A = \{i\}\), \(B = \{j\}\), \(C = V \setminus \{i,j\}\)). By the Hammersley–Clifford theorem, for positive distributions the two are equivalent.
State the Hammersley–Clifford theorem.	For a positive distribution (full support), the following are equivalent: (1) the distribution is pairwise Markov w.r.t. \(G\), (2) the distribution is global Markov w.r.t. \(G\), (3) the density factorizes over the cliques of \(G\): \(f(x) \propto \prod_{k=1}^{r} \psi_{C_k}(x_{C_k})\). This connects graph separation with probabilistic factorization.
What is a clique in a graph, and what role do cliques play in the Hammersley–Clifford factorization?	A clique is a maximal fully connected subgraph (every pair of vertices in the subset is connected by an edge). Hammersley–Clifford says the joint density factors as a product of potential functions, one for each clique: \(f(x) \propto \prod_{k=1}^{r} \psi_{C_k}(x_{C_k})\). Each potential \(\psi_{C_k}\) depends only on the variables in clique \(C_k\).
Show how factorization implies CI in the chain graph \(1 - 2 - 3\).	Cliques are \(\{1,2\}\) and \(\{2,3\}\), so \(f(x_1, x_2, x_3) \propto \psi_{12}(x_1, x_2)\,\psi_{23}(x_2, x_3)\). Fixing \(x_2\), the conditional \(f(x_1, x_3 \mid x_2)\) separates into a function of \(x_1\) alone times a function of \(x_3\) alone, giving \(f(x_1, x_3 \mid x_2) = f(x_1 \mid x_2)\,f(x_3 \mid x_2)\), hence \(X_1 \perp\!\!\!\perp X_3 \mid X_2\).
Define the Gaussian graphical model \(\mathcal{M}(G)\).	Given an undirected graph \(G = (V, E)\), the Gaussian graphical model is \(\mathcal{M}(G) = \{N_m(\mu, \Sigma) : K_{ij} = 0 \text{ whenever } i \not\sim j\}\), where \(K = \Sigma^{-1}\). It is the set of all multivariate normal distributions whose precision matrix has zeros matching the missing edges of \(G\).
State the key equivalence that makes Gaussian graphical models tractable.	For Gaussian data: \(i \not\sim j \iff K_{ij} = 0 \iff X_i \perp\!\!\!\perp X_j \mid X_{V \setminus \{i,j\}}\). Learning the graph is the same as learning the zero pattern of the precision matrix \(K = \Sigma^{-1}\).
Why does the Gaussian density automatically satisfy the positivity condition required by Hammersley–Clifford?	The Gaussian density \(f(x) = (2\pi)^{-m/2}|\Sigma|^{-1/2}\exp\!\left(-\tfrac{1}{2}(x-\mu)^\top \Sigma^{-1}(x-\mu)\right)\) is strictly positive for all \(x \in \mathbb{R}^m\) (the exponential is always positive). So the full-support condition is automatically met, and pairwise Markov, global Markov, and clique factorization are all equivalent.
Write the profile log-likelihood for the Gaussian graphical model in terms of \(K = \Sigma^{-1}\).	Given i.i.d. data with sample covariance \(S = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{k=1}^n (X^{(k)} - \bar{X})(X^{(k)} - \bar{X})^\top\), the profile log-likelihood (up to a constant) is \(\ell(K) = \frac{n}{2}\left(\log \det K - \operatorname{tr}(SK)\right)\).
What is the unconstrained MLE of the precision matrix \(K\)?	If there are no graph constraints and \(S\) is invertible, the MLE is \(\hat{K} = S^{-1}\). This is found by setting the gradient of \(\ell(K) = \frac{n}{2}(\log \det K - \operatorname{tr}(SK))\) to zero, which gives \(K^{-1} = S\).
How is the constrained MLE computed when the graph \(G\) is known?	Maximize the Gaussian log-likelihood subject to zero constraints: \(\hat{K}_G = \arg\max_{K \in S_+^m,\; K_{ij}=0 \text{ if } i \not\sim j} \left\{\log \det K - \operatorname{tr}(SK)\right\}\). There is a unique optimizer, and efficient iterative algorithms (e.g., iterative proportional fitting) exist to find it.
What are the two distinct problems in Gaussian graphical modeling?	1) Estimation: given a known graph \(G\), estimate \(K\) under the zero constraints. 2) Structure learning: when \(G\) is unknown, search over possible zero patterns to find the graph. Structure learning is the harder problem because the number of graphs on \(m\) vertices is \(2^{\binom{m}{2}}\), making exhaustive search infeasible.
How many undirected graphs exist on \(m\) vertices, and what does this imply?	\(2^{\binom{m}{2}}\). For \(m = 20\), this is \(2^{190} \approx 10^{57}\). Exhaustive enumeration is impossible even for moderate \(m\), so structure learning requires regularization or greedy/penalized approaches rather than brute-force search.
Why does naive covariance inversion fail in high dimensions?	When \(m\) is large relative to \(n\): (1) \(S\) is noisy, (2) \(S^{-1}\) is unstable (small eigenvalue errors get amplified), (3) if \(m > n\), \(S\) is singular and \(S^{-1}\) does not exist. This is the same fundamental problem as high-dimensional regression: too many parameters, not enough data.
What is the analogy between LASSO regression and graphical LASSO?	LASSO regression: estimate \(\beta \in \mathbb{R}^p\) with \(\ell_1\) penalty to encourage sparsity in coefficients. Graphical LASSO: estimate precision matrix \(K \in S_+^m\) with \(\ell_1\) penalty on off-diagonal entries to encourage sparsity (i.e., missing edges). Both impose sparsity to handle high-dimensional settings where \(p\) or \(m\) is large relative to \(n\).
Write the graphical LASSO objective function and explain each term.	\(\hat{K}_\lambda = \arg\max_{K \succ 0} \left\{\log \det K - \operatorname{tr}(SK) - \lambda \sum_{i \neq j} |K_{ij}|\right\}\). First term: rewards large determinant (well-conditioned \(K\)). Second term: data fit (likelihood). Third term: \(\ell_1\) penalty that shrinks off-diagonal entries toward zero, producing a sparse graph. \(\lambda > 0\) controls the sparsity level.
What is the role of the penalty parameter \(\lambda\) in graphical LASSO?	\(\lambda\) controls sparsity. Larger \(\lambda\) shrinks more off-diagonal entries of \(K\) to exactly zero, producing a sparser graph (fewer edges). Smaller \(\lambda\) allows more edges. There is no single "correct" graph — the result depends on \(\lambda\), chosen via cross-validation, BIC, EBIC, or stability methods.
Give three reasons why \(\ell_1\) penalization is useful in graphical models.	1) Statistical: reduces variance and stabilizes estimation when \(m\) is large. 2) Economic: encodes the belief that most variable pairs are not directly linked after controlling for others. 3) Interpretive: produces sparse networks that can actually be read, discussed, and visualized meaningfully.
What methods are commonly used to choose \(\lambda\) in practice?	Cross-validation, information criteria (BIC, EBIC), and stability-based methods. EBIC (Extended BIC) is particularly convenient and is implemented in R packages <code>qgraph</code> and <code>huge</code>. The choice of method affects the resulting graph.
What does <code>huge(x, method = "glasso")</code> compute in R?	It fits the graphical LASSO for many values of \(\lambda\) along a regularization path, producing a sequence of estimated precision matrices ranging from dense graphs (small \(\lambda\)) to sparse graphs (large \(\lambda\)). The output <code>out$icov[[k]]</code> selects the \(k\)-th estimate along this path.
Why is Gaussianity often too optimistic for economic data?	Asset returns are heavy-tailed, macro/firm-level variables may be skewed, relationships can be nonlinear, and monotone rescalings can substantially change Pearson correlations. All of these violate the Gaussian assumption, potentially distorting the estimated graph structure.
What is the non-paranormal model?	\(X = (X_1, \ldots, X_m)\) is non-paranormal if there exist strictly monotone functions \(f_1, \ldots, f_m\) such that \((f_1(X_1), \ldots, f_m(X_m)) \sim N_m(\mu, \Sigma)\). The observed variables are monotone transformations of latent Gaussian variables. This relaxes Gaussian marginals while preserving Gaussian graphical structure.
Why do monotone transformations preserve the conditional independence graph?	If \(Y_i = f_i(X_i)\) with each \(f_i\) strictly monotone, then \(X_i \perp\!\!\!\perp X_j \mid X_{V \setminus \{i,j\}} \iff Y_i \perp\!\!\!\perp Y_j \mid Y_{V \setminus \{i,j\}}\). Monotone transformations may change means, variances, and Pearson correlations, but they do not alter the conditional independence structure. So the graph of the latent Gaussians is the same as the graph of the observed variables.
Define Kendall's tau \(\tau_{ij}\).	\(\tau_{ij} = P\!\left((X_i - \tilde{X}_i)(X_j - \tilde{X}_j) > 0\right) - P\!\left((X_i - \tilde{X}_i)(X_j - \tilde{X}_j) < 0\right)\), where \((\tilde{X}_i, \tilde{X}_j)\) is an independent copy of \((X_i, X_j)\). It measures the probability of concordance minus the probability of discordance — a rank-based measure of association.
What is the crucial invariance property of Kendall's tau?	\(\tau(f_i(X_i), f_j(X_j)) = \tau(X_i, X_j)\) for any strictly monotone functions \(f_i, f_j\). Kendall's tau is invariant under monotone transformations because it depends only on the ranks (ordering) of the data, not the actual values.
State the bridge formula connecting Kendall's tau to Gaussian correlation.	If \((X_i, X_j)\) is bivariate Gaussian with correlation \(\Sigma_{ij}\), then \(\tau_{ij} = \frac{2}{\pi}\arcsin(\Sigma_{ij})\), equivalently \(\Sigma_{ij} = \sin\!\left(\frac{\pi}{2}\tau_{ij}\right)\). This allows recovery of the latent Gaussian correlation from the observed rank-based statistic.
How does the bridge formula enable estimation under the non-paranormal model?	If the observed \(Y_i = f_i(X_i)\) with unknown monotone \(f_i\) and latent \(X \sim N_m(\mu, \Sigma)\), then \(\tau(Y_i, Y_j) = \tau(X_i, X_j)\) by monotone invariance. So the latent Gaussian correlation is \(\Sigma_{ij} = \sin\!\left(\frac{\pi}{2}\,\tau(Y_i, Y_j)\right)\). We recover the latent correlation matrix without ever knowing the transformations \(f_i\).
Describe the three-step procedure for estimating a non-paranormal graph.	1) Compute the matrix of sample Kendall's taus \(\hat{\tau}_{ij}\). 2) Convert to a latent correlation estimate: \(\hat{S}_{ij}^\tau = \sin\!\left(\frac{\pi}{2}\,\hat{\tau}_{ij}\right)\). 3) Apply a standard Gaussian graph-learning method (e.g., graphical LASSO) to \(\hat{S}^\tau\). Estimate the latent Gaussian correlation first, then estimate the sparse graph.
What does <code>huge.npn(x, npn.func = "skeptic")</code> do in R?	It applies a rank-based non-paranormal transformation to the data. The <code>"skeptic"</code> option uses Kendall's tau to estimate the latent correlation matrix via the \(\sin(\frac{\pi}{2}\hat{\tau})\) bridge formula. The transformed data can then be passed to <code>huge()</code> with <code>method = "glasso"</code> for sparse graph estimation.
Compare the pure Gaussian approach vs. the non-paranormal approach for network estimation.	Gaussian: uses sample covariance/correlation directly. Fast and elegant but sensitive to heavy tails and marginal distortions. Non-paranormal: uses rank information (Kendall's tau) to recover a latent Gaussian correlation structure. More robust when marginals are non-Gaussian. For economic data, the robustness to monotone distortions is usually worth the extra step.
What can an estimated undirected graph tell us?	It can identify clusters of directly related variables, separate broad marginal comovement from direct conditional links, generate hypotheses about sectors/channels/transmission mechanisms, and reduce a high-dimensional system to a manageable network summary. Example: stock return graphs reveal sectoral clusters, central firms, and weak cross-sector connections.
What can an estimated undirected graph NOT tell us?	It cannot provide causal direction, intervention effects, or economic mechanisms by itself. An undirected edge means "conditional association remains" — it does NOT mean "\(i\) causes \(j\)." Moving from association to causation requires directed graphs (DAGs), which are covered in subsequent lectures.
Why is learning the sparsity pattern the real challenge in Gaussian graphical models?	Given a known graph, the constrained MLE has a unique solution with efficient algorithms. But finding the graph requires searching over \(2^{\binom{m}{2}}\) possible zero patterns. The graphical LASSO sidesteps this combinatorial explosion by continuously shrinking off-diagonal entries, effectively performing estimation and structure learning simultaneously.
What is the economic interpretation of a sparse estimated graph for asset returns?	Edges represent pairs of assets that remain associated after controlling for all other assets. Missing edges mean those pairs are conditionally independent. Clusters may correspond to sectors. Central nodes (high-degree vertices) may represent systemically important assets. Cross-cluster edges may reveal contagion or transmission channels beyond sectoral comovement.
In the sample covariance experiment with \(m = 100\) and \(n = 80\), what goes wrong?	With \(m > n\), the sample covariance matrix \(S\) is singular (rank at most \(n < m\)), so \(S^{-1}\) does not exist. Even when \(m < n\) but \(m\) is close to \(n\), eigenvalues of \(S\) are poorly estimated, making \(S^{-1}\) numerically unstable. This is exactly the regime where network methods (graphical LASSO) are most needed.
What is the conceptual difference between the covariance matrix \(\Sigma\) and the precision matrix \(K = \Sigma^{-1}\) in network terms?	\(\Sigma_{ij} \neq 0\): variables \(i\) and \(j\) are marginally associated (may be through indirect paths). \(K_{ij} \neq 0\): variables \(i\) and \(j\) are conditionally associated (direct link remains after controlling for all others). The covariance captures total association; the precision captures direct association. Dense covariance with sparse precision means most association is indirect.
What is the main lesson of Lecture 2?	Conditional independence becomes a network, and sparsity makes that network learnable. In Gaussian models, missing edges correspond to zeros in \(K = \Sigma^{-1}\). In high dimensions, the graphical LASSO imposes \(\ell_1\) sparsity on \(K\). Non-paranormal methods extend this to non-Gaussian data via rank-based estimation of latent Gaussian correlations.
Why is the Hammersley–Clifford theorem important for applied work?	It guarantees that for positive distributions, we can freely move between three equivalent characterizations: (1) pairwise CI from missing edges, (2) global CI from graph separation, and (3) density factorization over cliques. This means reading CI from the graph is fully justified, and the graph encodes the complete CI structure of the model.
What is the regularization path in the context of graphical LASSO?	It is a sequence of estimated precision matrices \(\hat{K}_{\lambda_1}, \hat{K}_{\lambda_2}, \ldots\) computed for a grid of decreasing \(\lambda\) values. Small \(\lambda\) gives dense graphs (many edges), large \(\lambda\) gives sparse graphs (few edges). The path lets you visualize how the network structure changes with the level of regularization.
