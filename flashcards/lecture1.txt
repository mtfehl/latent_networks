What is a joint distribution of \((X, Y)\)?	It describes the full probabilistic behaviour of \((X, Y)\) together. Continuous: density \(f_{XY}(x,y)\). Discrete: \(P(X=x, Y=y)\). It captures how variables move together (e.g., inflation & unemployment, returns on two assets).
How do you obtain the marginal distribution of \(X\) from the joint \(f_{XY}(x, y)\)?	Continuous: \(f_X(x) = \int f_{XY}(x,y) \, dy\). Discrete: \(f_X(x) = \sum_y f_{XY}(x,y)\). You "integrate out" or "sum out" the other variable.
What is the conditional distribution \(f_{Y|X}(y|x)\)?	\(f_{Y|X}(y|x) = \frac{f_{XY}(x,y)}{f_X(x)}\), for \(f_X(x) > 0\). It describes the distribution of \(Y\) when \(X\) is known to take value \(x\). Economically: restricting to a subpopulation (e.g., among treated units, among firms in distress).
In the fraud detection example (90% sensitivity, 90% specificity, 1% prevalence), what is \(P(F \mid +)\)?	\(P(F \mid +) = \frac{0.009}{0.009 + 0.099} \approx 0.083\) or 8.3%. Even a strong test produces many false alarms when the base rate is low. This illustrates the base rate fallacy: \(P(F \mid +) \neq P(+ \mid F)\).
What is the formal definition of independence \(X \perp\!\!\!\perp Y\)?	\(f_{XY}(x,y) = f_X(x) \cdot f_Y(y)\) for all \(x, y\). Equivalently: \(f_{Y|X}(y|x) = f_Y(y)\). Knowing \(X\) does not change the distribution of \(Y\) and gives zero predictive power over \(Y\).
What is mean independence, and how does it relate to full independence?	Mean independence: \(E[Y \mid X] = E[Y]\) and \(E[X \mid Y] = E[X]\). It is strictly weaker than independence — the conditional mean doesn't change, but higher moments (variance, skewness) still might. Example: heteroskedasticity where \(X\) affects the spread of \(Y\) but not the center.
What is conditional independence \(X \perp\!\!\!\perp Y \mid Z\)?	\(f_{XY|Z}(x,y \mid z) = f_{X|Z}(x \mid z) \cdot f_{Y|Z}(y \mid z)\) for all \(x, y, z\). Equivalently: \(f_{Y|XZ}(y \mid x,z) = f_{Y|Z}(y \mid z)\). Once \(Z\) is known, learning \(X\) gives no additional information about \(Y\). It captures the absence of a direct link.
What is the unconfoundedness assumption in causal inference, expressed as CI?	\((Y(1), Y(0)) \perp\!\!\!\perp D \mid X\). Given observed covariates \(X\), treatment assignment \(D\) is as good as random with respect to potential outcomes. This is a conditional independence statement.
In the model \(Y = \beta X + \gamma Z + \varepsilon\) with \(\varepsilon \perp\!\!\!\perp (X,Z)\), what does \(\beta = 0\) imply?	\(Y \perp\!\!\!\perp X \mid Z\) (full conditional independence). \(X\) drops out of the DGP once \(Z\) is controlled for. But if we only assume \(E[\varepsilon \mid X,Z] = 0\), then \(\beta = 0\) only implies mean independence of \(Y\) from \(X\) given \(Z\), not full CI.
What is \(E[Y \mid X]\) and why is it called the regression function?	\(E[Y \mid X = x] = \int y \, f_{Y|X}(y \mid x) \, dy\). It is the best predictor of \(Y\) from \(X\) under squared loss — i.e., it minimizes \(E[(Y - g(X))^2]\) over all functions \(g\). This is why regression is so central.
State the density of the multivariate normal \(X \sim N_m(\mu, \Sigma)\).	\(f(x) = (2\pi)^{-m/2} |\Sigma|^{-1/2} \exp\!\left(-\tfrac{1}{2} (x-\mu)^\top \Sigma^{-1} (x-\mu)\right)\), where \(\mu \in \mathbb{R}^m\) is the mean vector and \(\Sigma\) is the \(m \times m\) positive definite covariance matrix.
What is the marginal distribution of \(X_A\) when \(X = (X_A, X_B)^\top \sim N(\mu, \Sigma)\) partitioned conformably?	\(X_A \sim N(\mu_A, \Sigma_{AA})\). Just read off the relevant subblocks. No integration needed — Gaussian families are closed under marginalization.
What is the conditional distribution \(X_A \mid X_B = x_B\) for a jointly Gaussian vector?	Gaussian with: \(E[X_A \mid X_B = x_B] = \mu_A + \Sigma_{AB} \Sigma_{BB}^{-1} (x_B - \mu_B)\). \(\text{Var}(X_A \mid X_B = x_B) = \Sigma_{AA} - \Sigma_{AB} \Sigma_{BB}^{-1} \Sigma_{BA}\). Key: the conditional mean is linear in \(x_B\), and the conditional variance does NOT depend on \(x_B\).
For jointly Gaussian vectors, what is special about zero covariance?	\(X_A \perp\!\!\!\perp X_B \iff \Sigma_{AB} = 0\). For general distributions, zero covariance does NOT imply independence. For Gaussians, it does. This is a major simplification.
What is the precision matrix and what does it capture?	\(K = \Sigma^{-1}\) (the inverse of the covariance matrix). The covariance matrix describes marginal dependence (do \(X_i\) and \(X_j\) co-move?). The precision matrix describes conditional dependence (is there a direct link after controlling for everything else?).
State the key CI result for precision matrices under Gaussianity.	For \(X \sim N_m(\mu, \Sigma)\) with \(K = \Sigma^{-1}\): \(X_i \perp\!\!\!\perp X_j \mid X_{\{1,\ldots,m\} \setminus \{i,j\}} \iff K_{ij} = 0\). A zero off-diagonal entry in \(K\) means no direct relation between \(X_i\) and \(X_j\) once all other variables are controlled for. This is the bridge to graphical models (zero = missing edge).
What is the conditional variance in terms of the precision matrix?	If \(K\) is partitioned as \(K = \begin{pmatrix} K_{AA} & K_{AB} \\ K_{BA} & K_{BB} \end{pmatrix}\), then \(\text{Var}(X_A \mid X_B) = K_{AA}^{-1}\). This comes from block matrix inversion and the Schur complement.
What is the partial correlation \(\rho_{X,Y \cdot Z}\) and what does it measure?	\(\rho_{X,Y \cdot Z} = \frac{\rho_{XY} - \rho_{XZ}\,\rho_{YZ}}{\sqrt{(1-\rho_{XZ}^2)(1-\rho_{YZ}^2)}}\). It measures the residual linear association between \(X\) and \(Y\) after removing the linear effect of \(Z\). Equivalently: \(\rho_{X,Y \cdot Z} = 0\) iff the coefficient of \(Y\) is zero in a regression of \(X\) on \(Y\) and \(Z\).
Under Gaussianity, what is the relationship between partial correlation and CI?	Zero partial correlation \(\iff\) conditional independence. For non-Gaussian data, zero partial correlation only captures the linear component, and CI may fail even when the partial correlation is zero.
What is the economic interpretation of a sparse precision matrix vs. a dense covariance matrix?	Dense covariance: many variables co-move marginally (often due to common shocks). Sparse precision: after controlling for everything else, only a few pairs have direct links. The sparsity reveals the actual wiring (contagion channels, input-output links, interbank dependence) stripped of indirect associations.
What is Simpson's paradox?	A marginal association can reverse direction when you condition on a third variable. The aggregate data suggests one conclusion, but within every subgroup defined by the confounder, the opposite holds. It arises when a confounding variable affects both treatment assignment and outcomes.
Explain the kidney stone treatment example (Simpson's paradox).	Overall: treatment \(b\) has higher recovery (83% vs 78%). But for small stones: \(a\) is better (93% vs 87%). For large stones: \(a\) is also better (73% vs 69%). Stone size is a confounder — large stones are harder to treat AND more likely assigned to treatment \(a\). The marginal comparison conflates treatment effect with case severity.
Explain the UC Berkeley admissions example (Simpson's paradox).	Overall: 44% of men vs 35% of women admitted. But department-by-department, women were admitted at equal or higher rates. Women disproportionately applied to more competitive (lower-acceptance) departments, creating a compositional effect that reversed the sign.
What is Berkson's paradox?	Conditioning on a common effect (collider) of two independent variables can create spurious dependence. Even if \(X \perp\!\!\!\perp Y\) in the population, \(X \not\!\perp\!\!\!\perp Y \mid S\) where \(S\) depends on both \(X\) and \(Y\).
Explain the dating pool example of Berkson's paradox.	Looks (\(X\)) and Personality (\(Y\)) are independent in the population. You only date people who are attractive OR nice (\(S\) depends on \(X+Y\)). Within your dating pool, \(X\) and \(Y\) become negatively correlated: if someone isn't attractive, they must be very nice to be in the pool.
Give two economic examples of Berkson's paradox.	1) Wage vs. job satisfaction among workers who stay in a demanding sector — low-wage stayers must have high satisfaction, creating negative correlation. 2) Ability vs. family background among students admitted to a selective program — weak-background admits must have very high ability.
In a mediation structure \(X \to Z \to Y\) with \(Z = aX + \varepsilon_Z\), \(Y = bZ + \varepsilon_Y\), all noise independent, what CI relationships hold?	\(X\) and \(Y\) are marginally dependent (through the path via \(Z\), since \(Y = abX + b\varepsilon_Z + \varepsilon_Y\)). But \(Y \perp\!\!\!\perp X \mid Z\) — conditioning on the mediator blocks the indirect path. Regressing \(Y\) on \(X\) alone captures the total effect \(ab\).
In a confounding structure \(X \leftarrow U \rightarrow Y\) with \(X = aU + \varepsilon_X\), \(Y = bU + \varepsilon_Y\), noise independent, what CI relationships hold?	\(X\) and \(Y\) are marginally dependent (spuriously, through \(U\)). But \(X \perp\!\!\!\perp Y \mid U\) — conditioning on the confounder removes the spurious association. \(U\) is the omitted variable; failing to control for it biases inference.
In a collider/selection structure \(X \to S \leftarrow Y\) with \(S = X + Y + \varepsilon\), what CI relationships hold?	\(X \perp\!\!\!\perp Y\) in the population (they are independent). But conditioning on \(S\) (e.g., \(S > 0\)) can induce dependence: \(\text{cor}(X, Y \mid S > 0) \neq 0\). Selection is often an implicit conditioning step in empirical work.
What three structural patterns describe how conditioning affects dependence?	1) Mediation (\(X \to Z \to Y\)): conditioning on \(Z\) blocks indirect path, removes dependence. 2) Confounding (\(X \leftarrow U \rightarrow Y\)): conditioning on \(U\) removes spurious association. 3) Collider (\(X \to S \leftarrow Y\)): conditioning on \(S\) creates dependence that wasn't there.
What does Pearson's correlation test assess, and when is it valid?	It tests \(H_0: \rho = 0\) vs \(H_A: \rho \neq 0\). Valid when dependence is approximately linear and data are roughly Gaussian. It tests for vanishing linear association only — NOT for full independence.
What is Fisher's z-transform and why is it used?	\(Z_n = \tfrac{1}{2} \log\!\left(\frac{1+r_n}{1-r_n}\right)\). Under bivariate normality, \(Z_n \sim N\!\left(\tfrac{1}{2}\log\!\left(\frac{1+\rho}{1-\rho}\right),\; \frac{1}{n-3}\right)\) approximately. It stabilizes the variance of the sample correlation (which otherwise depends on \(\rho\)), enabling standard normal-based inference. This is what <code>cor.test()</code> in R uses.
Why can Pearson correlation miss strong dependence? Give an example.	Pearson only measures the linear component. The rotated uniform example: \(X\) and \(Y\) are deterministic functions of the same underlying variables (clearly dependent) but are uncorrelated (\(r \approx 0\), \(p = 0.398\)). Any symmetric or nonlinear dependence pattern can produce zero correlation.
What is distance correlation and what key property does it have?	Distance correlation \(\mathcal{R}(X,Y)\) is a fully nonparametric measure with the property: \(\mathcal{R}(X,Y) = 0 \iff X \perp\!\!\!\perp Y\). Unlike Pearson, it detects any form of dependence (nonlinear, non-monotone). Tested via permutation with <code>dcor.test()</code> from the <code>energy</code> package in R.
In the aircraft example, what do Pearson and distance correlation find?	Pearson: \(p = 0.78\) (no evidence of linear association). Distance correlation: \(p < 0.001\) (strong evidence of dependence). The relationship between wingspan and speed is nonlinear, so Pearson misses it entirely.
How is independence tested for discrete data?	Using the \(\chi^2\) test: compare observed cell counts to expected counts under independence (expected = row total \(\times\) col total / grand total). Degrees of freedom = \((\text{rows}-1)(\text{cols}-1)\). In R: <code>chisq.test()</code>.
Why is testing conditional independence (CI) harder than testing marginal independence?	You must compare conditional distributions rather than marginal ones. For Gaussian data: reduces to testing zero partial correlation. For discrete data: stratified \(\chi^2\) or likelihood-ratio tests. For nonlinear/non-Gaussian continuous data: need kernel methods (KCI) which are computationally expensive. Difficulty depends strongly on modeling assumptions.
What R packages/functions test conditional independence?	<code>bnlearn::ci.test()</code> — general CI testing (supports \(\chi^2\), mutual info, etc.). <code>CondIndTests::CondIndTest()</code> with <code>method="KCI"</code> — kernel CI test for nonlinear, non-Gaussian data. <code>gRim::ciTest()</code> — CI testing for discrete data with slice-level detail.
In the Berkeley admissions CI test (\(\text{Gender} \perp\!\!\!\perp \text{Admit} \mid \text{Dept}\)), what do the tests find?	Both <code>bnlearn::ci.test</code> and <code>gRim::ciTest</code> return \(p = 1\). There is no evidence of direct gender bias once department is conditioned on. The apparent overall disparity was entirely driven by differential application patterns across departments (Simpson's paradox).
What is the core message of Lecture 1?	Conditional independence + graphs + latent variables = scalable structure for modern empirical work. To understand dependence, always ask what is being conditioned on. Conditioning can remove dependence (mediation, confounding) or create it (collider/Berkson's). In Gaussian models, CI becomes algebraic through the precision matrix.
What is the "Gaussian bridge" in this lecture?	The Gaussian distribution bridges regression and conditional independence. Under Gaussianity: (1) conditional distributions are Gaussian with linear mean, (2) zero covariance \(\Rightarrow\) independence, (3) zeros in the precision matrix \(K\) correspond to CI, (4) partial correlation zero \(\Leftrightarrow\) CI. This makes CI questions into algebra.
What does a non-significant Pearson test result tell you?	It does NOT imply independence. It only means there is insufficient evidence to reject zero linear correlation. Dependence may still exist in nonlinear forms. A non-significant result is not evidence of absence.
What is the connection between the precision matrix and graphical models?	In an undirected Gaussian graphical model, each zero off-diagonal entry \(K_{ij} = 0\) in the precision matrix corresponds to a missing edge between nodes \(i\) and \(j\). Missing edge = conditional independence of \(X_i\) and \(X_j\) given all other variables. This encodes the full CI structure visually.
What is the practical distinction between covariance and precision in macro/finance?	Covariance: shows many variables move together (e.g., all stocks fall in a crisis) — reflects common shocks and indirect channels. Precision: shows which pairs are directly linked after netting out everything else — reveals actual contagion pathways, input-output links, or interbank exposures.
State the formula for conditional variance using the Schur complement.	For partitioned Gaussian: \(\text{Var}(X_A \mid X_B) = \Sigma_{AA} - \Sigma_{AB} \Sigma_{BB}^{-1} \Sigma_{BA}\). Equivalently (from the precision matrix): \(\text{Var}(X_A \mid X_B) = K_{AA}^{-1}\). The Schur complement connects the two representations.
Why does the direction of conditioning matter? Give the fraud example intuition.	\(P(+ \mid F) = 0.9\) (test catches 90% of fraud) but \(P(F \mid +) \approx 0.083\) (only 8.3% of flags are fraud). Conditioning "test result given fraud" vs "fraud given test result" are very different questions. When the base rate is low, even accurate tests produce mostly false positives. Bayes' theorem connects the two directions.
What is the general lesson about selection bias from Part 3?	Conditioning on a consequence (collider) can create dependence that was not there before. Selection is often an implicit conditioning step in empirical data (survival bias, sample selection, truncation). Variables may be unrelated in the full population but become associated in the selected subsample.
What does \(\beta = 0\) in a linear model imply under full noise independence vs. only mean exogeneity?	Under \(\varepsilon \perp\!\!\!\perp (X,Z)\): \(\beta = 0 \Rightarrow Y \perp\!\!\!\perp X \mid Z\) (full conditional independence). Under only \(E[\varepsilon \mid X,Z] = 0\): \(\beta = 0 \Rightarrow E[Y \mid X,Z]\) does not depend on \(X\) (mean independence only). The gap matters when higher moments carry economic content (risk, inequality, heteroskedasticity).
